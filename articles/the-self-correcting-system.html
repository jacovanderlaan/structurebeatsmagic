The Self-Correcting System

A system that gets better on its own does two opposite things at once: it grows by remembering its mistakes, and it stays sharp by forgetting almost everything. Most people build one and wonder why it rots.

By Jaco van der Laan · 2026-07-17

The brief (why this article, in one breath)

Ask what makes a system get better on its own and you get a paradox back. It has to grow — accumulate what it learns so yesterday's mistake isn't repeated tomorrow. And it has to shrink — hand any single task only the slice that task needs, or it drowns in its own accumulation. Grow and prune. Most people build only the first half: they add and add — more notes, more rules, more context — and then wonder why the thing gets slower and vaguer instead of smarter. The missing insight is that a self-correcting system runs on two opposite motions at once, and they're not in conflict; they're the same discipline seen from both ends. It grows where it remembers and shrinks where it acts.

Core argument (skeleton — to write)

1. The paradox up front. State it cleanly: "get better over time" secretly demands two contradictory behaviours — accumulate (so nothing is re-learned) and discard (so nothing is drowned). Name the common failure: people only do the first, and a system that only grows doesn't compound — it congeals. (Collecting Is Not Capability is the same failure wearing a PKM hat.)

2. The growth axis — remembering by correction. Introduce, don't re-teach: the unit of growth isn't "more content," it's the correction. Same mistake twice → a new rule the system reads every run (The Mistakes File — link out for the mechanism). The key property to carry forward: growth is selective. You don't accumulate everything that happens; you accumulate the residue of failures that must not repeat. Congealed feedback, not a diary.

3. The pruning axis — acting by cutting. The opposite motion: at the moment of doing, the system does NOT pour its whole accumulated memory into the task. It cuts a slice — the context this decision actually needs — and reasons over that (The Context Graph: small enough to reason across, not search through — link out). A system that hands every task its entire memory has organised a haystack and then made itself search it.

4. Why the two are the same discipline. The synthesis — the article's real contribution. Both axes are answering one question from opposite sides: what deserves to persist, and what deserves to be present right now? Growth decides what's worth keeping forever (the correction that generalises). Pruning decides what's worth loading for this one task (the slice that's relevant now). Keep-forever and load-now are not the same set — and confusing them is the root disease. People persist what they should have loaded once (clutter) and load what they should have persisted (re-explaining themselves every session). Sort the two and the system breathes.

5. The shape it makes — a figure eight, not a pile. The mental model. A pile only grows; it has one motion and it ends as a mound. A self-correcting system is a loop with two lobes: corrections flow in on one side (it gets bigger where it remembers), scoped slices flow out on the other (it gets smaller where it acts). Same loop, opposite motions at each turn. That two-stroke rhythm is what "compounding" actually looks like from the inside — not a swelling archive, but a system that is heavier in memory and lighter in action every week.

6. The practical audit. Turn it into something the reader does. Two questions, run periodically: (a) Growth check — what have I corrected more than once and never written down? (those are ungrown rules leaking value.) (b) Prune check — what am I loading into every task that this task doesn't need? (that's un-pruned context slowing every answer.) A healthy system fails neither check for long. Most unhealthy ones fail both — over-remembering the trivial, under-remembering the load-bearing.

7. Close — better on an ordinary Tuesday. The mark of a self-correcting system isn't that it's big. It's that it's demonstrably better this week than last, without anyone rebuilding it — because it quietly grew where it was corrected and stayed sharp where it acted. Structure beats magic; and the structure that improves itself is the two-stroke one: remember more, carry less.

Working notes

  • Tone: the satisfaction of a paradox resolving. Not "do more" — the opposite; the pruning half is the counter-cultural, memorable part.
  • Hard boundary: The Mistakes File and The Context Graph each get ~one paragraph as an on-ramp, then a link. The article is NOT those two cards stapled together — it's the tension and the sorting principle (keep-forever vs load-now) that neither card states.
  • One-liner candidates: "It gets bigger where it remembers and smaller where it acts." / "Keep-forever and load-now are different sets — confuse them and the system rots." / "A pile only grows; a system also cuts." / "Compounding isn't a swelling archive; it's a two-stroke loop."
  • Own proof (credit-in-passing): my own setup grows by a mechanical reflex (a correction becomes a rule the moment it recurs) and prunes by design (a routing layer hands each session a domain-slice, not the whole vault). The two together are why it's better on Tuesday than Monday.
  • Cluster note: this is the natural CAPSTONE of the method-wave — Define Only the Delta (what to capture) / Rules You Can Verify (is the rule checkable) / Hooks Not Hopes (is it enforced) / The Interview Is the Escalation (how context gets in) all feed the two-axis loop this article describes. Possible closer of a series.
