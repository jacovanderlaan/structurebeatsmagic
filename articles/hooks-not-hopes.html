Hooks, Not Hopes

Half your rules are wishes you're hoping the machine remembers. The other half run whether it remembers or not. The gap between them is the whole game.

By Jaco van der Laan · 2026-07-17

The brief (why this article, in one breath)

You wrote the rule down, so you think it's handled. "Always check the source before you trust it." "Never send without a review pass." "Always name files this way." Written once, in the standing-instructions file — and then quietly hoped into existence on every future run. But a rule you state and a rule you enforce are two different species. The first fires when the machine happens to remember it; the second fires whether it remembers or not. The whole reliability of a system comes down to how many of your rules are hooks — checkpoints that run automatically at a fixed moment — and how many are still hopes you're carrying in your head or burying in a document nobody re-reads under pressure. This is the rules pillar of Structure Beats Magic, brought down to personal scale: the difference between a system that behaves on a good day and one that behaves on a bad one.

Core argument (skeleton — to write)

1. Two species of rule. A hope is a rule you've expressed and are trusting to be honoured — an instruction in a prompt, a line in a doc, a good intention. A hook is a rule that is attached to a moment: it runs at a fixed trigger, automatically, without anyone choosing to invoke it. Same sentence, completely different reliability. The reader almost certainly has both and has never sorted them.

2. Why hopes fail exactly when you need them. Hoped rules degrade under the two conditions where rules matter most: volume (the doc grows, adherence drops — the twentieth rule is honoured less than the first) and pressure (tired, rushed, distracted — the human and the machine both skip the step). A rule that only holds when things are calm isn't a rule; it's a preference.

3. The prerequisite (nod, don't repeat). You can't hook a rule you can't check — see Rules You Can Verify. "Be thorough" can't be a hook because nothing can decide pass/fail at a trigger point. So the pipeline is: make the rule verifiable first, then attach it to a moment. Verifiability is the floor; enforcement is the wall you build on it.

4. What a hook looks like away from code. De-jargon the idea so it lands for a non-technical reader: a pre-commit check that won't let a broken file through; a form that refuses to submit until the required field is filled; a template that won't save with an empty title; a checklist the flow physically stops at. The pattern is always the same — the rule is fastened to a point in the flow, so obeying it isn't a decision anyone makes; it's a gate the flow passes through. "Human hits send" is a hook. "Remember to review" is a hope.

5. The move: promote your hopes. The practical heart of the article. Walk the reader through auditing their own standing rules and sorting each into hope or hook, then promoting the load-bearing ones. Not all of them — most rules can stay hopes. The discipline is spotting the few that must never fail (the ones where forgetting is expensive or irreversible) and giving those a fixed trigger instead of a line in a document. One promoted hook beats ten re-read reminders.

6. Why this is the forgotten pillar. Tie to The Missing System: data and AI get all the attention; rules are the third pillar nobody funds. And within rules, enforcement is the forgotten half — everyone writes rules, almost nobody attaches them to a moment. The systems that quietly work are the ones that moved their critical rules from the document into the machinery. This is where structure stops being a diagram and starts being something that holds.

7. Close — the good-day test. A system that only works when you're paying attention isn't a system; it's a performance. The question for every rule that matters: does this still hold on the day I forget it exists? If the answer depends on your memory, it's a hope. Make the few that matter into hooks — and let the machine keep the promise you won't always remember to.

Working notes

  • Tone: crisp, slightly wry ("a rule you hope into existence"). Permission to leave most rules as hopes — the point is triage, not maximalism. Avoid sounding like "automate everything."
  • One-liner candidates: "A rule you only state is a rule you're hoping for." / "Fasten the rule to a moment, not to your memory." / "Everyone writes rules; almost nobody bolts them down." / "Does it still hold on the day you forget it exists?"
  • Own proof (credit-in-passing, no vault tour): the strongest personal rules in my own setup aren't in a document I re-read — they're attached to triggers that fire on their own. The ones I only wrote down are the ones I still break.
  • Guard: keep "hook" generic. The moment it reads as a specific product feature, the evergreen value drops. A pre-commit check, a validating form, and a gated checklist are all hooks.
  • Relationship to siblings: this + Define Only the Delta + Rules You Can Verify form a tight method-cluster (what to capture / whether a rule is checkable / whether it's enforced). Could be pitched as a short series.
