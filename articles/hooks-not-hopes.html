Hooks, Not Hopes

Half your rules are wishes you're hoping the machine remembers. The other half run whether it remembers or not. The gap between them is the whole game.

By Jaco van der Laan · 2026-07-17

The brief (why this article, in one breath)

You wrote the rule down, so you think it's handled. "Always check the source before you trust it." "Never send without a review pass." "Always name files this way." Written once, in the standing-instructions file — and then quietly hoped into existence on every future run. But a rule you state and a rule you enforce are two different species. The first fires when the machine happens to remember it; the second fires whether it remembers or not. The whole reliability of a system comes down to how many of your rules are hooks — checkpoints that run automatically at a fixed moment — and how many are still hopes you're carrying in your head or burying in a document nobody re-reads under pressure. This is the rules pillar of Structure Beats Magic, brought down to personal scale: the difference between a system that behaves on a good day and one that behaves on a bad one.
