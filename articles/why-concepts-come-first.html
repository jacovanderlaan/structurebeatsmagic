Structure Beats Magic

Why Concepts Come First

A named idea is a coat rack. Give someone the rack in two minutes and every example afterwards has somewhere to hang — without it, each new detail lands on the floor.

By Jaco van der Laan · 2026-07-19

The coats on the floor

Sit through a talk dense with good examples and no frame, and something specific goes wrong. Each example makes sense as it arrives. None of them stack. An hour later you remember two anecdotes and no structure, because there was nothing for them to attach to.

That's what a missing concept costs. Not confusion in the moment — the sentences were fine. The loss is that nothing accumulated.

A concept is the rack. Give it to someone in two minutes and every example afterwards has a place to hang. They stop memorising your stories and start filing them onto a frame they now own.

Two jobs, and both are required

A concept has to do two things at once.

It fixes vocabulary. A term with a settled meaning can be reused without redefining it. When a piece says conformed dimension or anti-interests, it points at something that already carries precise meaning. The definitional work happens once, in one place, and everything downstream inherits it.

It gives a frame. Not just what the words mean, but the shape of the idea — what it's for, where it applies, what it excludes.

Drop either half and it stops working. Vocabulary without a frame is a glossary: accurate and inert. A frame without vocabulary is a feeling you can't hand to anyone else. The concept is both, and that's the entire job.

What this does to the writing

An article without an underlying concept has to build its own foundation every time: define the terms, establish the frame, then make the point. Slow to write, long to read, and impossible to connect to the next one.

Concepts absorb that cost. The frame already exists, so a piece opens at the point instead of the preamble. It inherits its foundation rather than pouring a new one.

The second effect matters more and takes longer to notice. When many articles hang on the same concept, they connect to each other through it. The concept is the shared joint. That's the difference between a body of work and a stack of unrelated posts — and it's structural, not editorial. It comes from the shape, not from cross-referencing harder.

In my own corpus that's 105 concepts underneath the articles, with 37 pieces carrying an explicit list of which concepts they stand on. Those aren't tags. They're the joints.

The two-minute test

Here's the rule that keeps a concept honest: you should be able to hand it over in about two minutes.

If it takes twenty, it isn't a concept. It's an article wearing a concept's name, and it will fail at the one job that mattered — being graspable fast enough to serve as a frame for what comes next.

Which implies a discipline that's harder than it sounds: concepts stay short and dense. Push the depth, the practice, the war stories out into the articles, where length is earned. The concept holds meaning and frame. The article hangs substance on it.

The temptation is always to enrich the concept note, because you know more than fits. Resist it. A concept that grows into an essay has stopped being usable at the exact moment it feels most complete.

Why this shows up hardest in a room

Everything above applies double when teaching.

Hand a group the frame in two minutes and every example afterwards attaches to something. Skip it, and you're asking people to hold your examples in working memory and infer the structure themselves — which some will, badly, and differently from each other.

This is why concept-first isn't a writing preference. It's the fastest way to make a room able to hold what comes next. The same property that makes an article shorter makes a workshop land.

The other half

There's a discipline that pairs with this one and pulls the opposite way, which is why both are needed.

Concepts-first says: name the idea before you use it. Concepts in Action says: a named idea earns its place only when you can point at it working in something real. Without the first, your examples don't stack. Without the second, your library drifts into clever-sounding abstraction — terms that feel like knowledge and demonstrate nothing.

Foundation first. Evidence after. A concept that has both is doing real work; one with neither is vocabulary cosplay.
