Highlights

  • Data becomes knowledge only when you manipulate it — Duhigg's "disfluency" finding (people who hand-process information retain and use it; passive dashboards get ignored) is the strongest scientific backing in the book for the second-brain practice: capture is worthless without engagement.
  • Mental models keep attention on course — people who narrate to themselves what should happen next spot anomalies faster; this is why an explicit model of your work (of a pipeline, of a day) outperforms reactive scrambling.
  • Team performance comes from norms, not talent — the Google Project Aristotle material shows psychological safety and shared norms beat individual brilliance, directly feeding the Smart Teams operating-model thesis: design the interaction rules, not the roster.
  • SMART goals need a stretch goal above them — decomposable near-term goals only produce movement when chained to an ambitious aim; a useful check on any planning structure that optimizes the checklist and forgets the intention.
  • Probabilistic thinking over certainty — good deciders hold multiple contradictory futures with rough odds attached, a decision discipline that pairs naturally with writing decision records that log options and rationale.
  • Motivation grows from making choices — the feeling of control, triggered by even small self-made decisions, drives sustained effort; systems should hand agency back to the person, not bury them in prescribed process.
