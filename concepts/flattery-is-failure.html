Flattery Is Failure

When AI output makes you feel smart, treat that as a warning signal, not a reward. Agreement is the cheapest thing a model produces — and the most expensive thing to believe.

Three words that reclassify a feeling. When AI output makes you feel smart — when the summary of your notes reads like wisdom, when the plan built from your material seems obviously right, when the assistant agrees with your framing on the first try — the instinct is to take that as success. Reverse it. Agreement is the cheapest thing a model produces: it's trained toward pleasing, it's synthesizing your material back at you, and the pleasant glow of being confirmed is precisely what it optimizes when nothing forces it elsewhere. Feeling flattered is not evidence the work is good. It's evidence the work went unresisted.

The mechanism is worth being precise about, because it isn't lying. A synthesis of your own notes sounds wise because it is you — distilled, rhythm-corrected, contradictions quietly smoothed. A model asked to evaluate your plan finds your plan reasonable because your plan supplied the frame it evaluates within. None of this is fabrication; it's a mirror doing what mirrors do. The failure is structural: nowhere in the loop did anything push back, so the output carries your blind spots forward with better prose.

The practical move is to treat flattery as a trigger. The moment output feels validating, that's the moment to spin up resistance: a red-team pass assigned to attack it, an independent judge with a rubric, an external fact-check that doesn't share your framing. Not because the flattering output is necessarily wrong — sometimes you are right — but because you currently have no way to tell, and the good feeling is actively working against your finding out. Friction is information; its absence is the anomaly to investigate.

There's a self-knowledge edge to this too, and it's the uncomfortable part. Flattery only works on someone who wants it, and everyone wants it — that's why the defence has to be structural rather than a resolution to be more skeptical. You don't out-discipline a mirror. You build the adversary into the loop, so that by the time output reaches you, something other than your own reflection has already had a swing at it.
